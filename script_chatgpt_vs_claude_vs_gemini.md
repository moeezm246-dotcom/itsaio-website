## SECTION 3 — TEST 1: WRITING QUALITY (1:30–3:00)

> **[TONE: Build curiosity. Deliver verdict with confidence.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Test one: writing.

I gave all three the same prompt — write a 500-word blog intro about why most people fail at building habits. No extra instructions. Just the prompt.

ChatGPT's version was... fine. Competent. It hit the structure you'd expect — hook, problem, promise. But it also had that unmistakable AI writing smell. The kind of thing where every sentence is technically correct but nothing lands. Words like "embark" and "journey" showed up. You know the ones.

Gemini went a different direction. More listicle-brained. It jumped straight into bullet points when I asked for flowing prose. The ideas were solid but the execution felt like it was optimized for a search engine, not a human reader.

Claude won this one. Not even close. It wrote like a person — with rhythm, with tension, with an actual point of view. When I ran the output through Originality.AI, it scored the lowest AI-detection score of the three. That matters if you're using AI for content work.

**Verdict: Claude for writing. ChatGPT second. Gemini third.**

## SECTION 4 — TEST 2: CODING (3:00–4:15)

> **[TONE: More technical but keep it accessible. Non-coders should still follow this.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Test two: coding.

I asked each AI to build a working Python script that scrapes the top 10 results from a Google search and saves them to a spreadsheet. This is a real task developers actually use AI for.

ChatGPT produced working code on the first try. Used the right libraries — BeautifulSoup, openpyxl. It even added error handling I didn't ask for. When the script hit Google's bot detection, it suggested a workaround without me asking.

Claude also produced clean code. Arguably better commented — you could hand it to a junior developer and they'd understand it. But it got tripped up by a library version conflict that ChatGPT handled smoothly.

Gemini gave me code that looked right but had a bug that caused it to silently fail — meaning it ran without crashing, but also without doing anything. That's the worst kind of bug. Took me 20 minutes to find it.

**Verdict: ChatGPT for coding. Claude second. Gemini third.**

## SECTION 5 — TEST 3: RESEARCH & ACCURACY (4:15–5:30)

> **[TONE: This is where you can be a bit dramatic — hallucination is a big deal.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Test three — and this one might be the most important: research and accuracy.

I asked all three the same question: "What were the five largest AI funding rounds of 2024, with amounts and investors?"

Gemini got this right. It has live web access by default and pulled accurate, sourced data. Dates were correct. Amounts matched what I verified independently.

ChatGPT was hit or miss. It got three of five right, confidently stated two figures that were wrong, and didn't flag any uncertainty. That's the dangerous kind of wrong — the kind that gets you embarrassed in a meeting.

Claude was honest. It said something like: "My training data has a cutoff and I can't verify current figures — here's what I know, but please verify." Less impressive-sounding. But correct. And when I gave it the real data and asked it to analyze trends, it produced the best analysis of the three.

The lesson here? For real-time factual research, Gemini wins. For analysis of information *you* provide, Claude wins. Never trust ChatGPT with facts it can't verify without double-checking.

**Verdict: Gemini for live research. Claude for analysis. ChatGPT — verify everything.**

## SECTION 6 — TEST 4: REASONING & LOGIC (5:30–6:45)

> **[TONE: More thoughtful. This is the "smart" section — slightly slower delivery.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Test four: complex reasoning.

I used a classic business case: a fictional company with five strategic options, limited budget, and conflicting stakeholder interests. I asked each AI to recommend a path and explain *why*.

ChatGPT with o3 access was genuinely impressive here. It laid out a decision tree, weighted trade-offs explicitly, and flagged assumptions. This is where the reasoning models earn their keep — and o3 is meaningfully smarter than GPT-4o on problems like this.

Claude held its own. It asked clarifying questions before answering, which felt annoying at first and then I realized it gave a much more tailored answer as a result. It also flagged a hidden constraint I'd buried in the prompt that the other two missed entirely.

Gemini felt surface-level on this one. It gave the "consultant answer" — comprehensive-sounding, non-committal, hedged in every direction. The kind of answer that technically says something but recommends nothing.

**Verdict: ChatGPT o3 for complex reasoning. Claude close second. Gemini third.**

## SECTION 7 — TEST 5: HANDLING LONG DOCUMENTS (6:45–7:45)

> **[TONE: Build this up — most people don't know about context windows. Make it feel like insider knowledge.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Test five: long documents. This one has a clear winner before I even run the test.

Claude has a 200,000 token context window. That means it can read and analyze a 500-page document in a single prompt. The entire works of Shakespeare — in one go.

ChatGPT's window is around 128,000 tokens. Gemini 1.5 Pro actually matches or beats Claude here, at up to one million tokens.

But here's the thing — having a big context window and *using it well* are different skills.

I fed all three the same 80-page legal contract and asked them to identify the five riskiest clauses.

Gemini found the most clauses but missed the context — it flagged boilerplate that wasn't actually risky.

ChatGPT found three genuinely risky clauses and explained them clearly.

Claude found five, ranked them by severity, quoted the exact page and line, and explained what leverage they gave the other party. An actual lawyer reviewed its output and said it was — quote — "better than a first-year associate."

**Verdict: Claude for long documents. It's not close.**

## SECTION 8 — THE REAL ANSWER (7:45–9:00)

> **[TONE: This is the payoff. Confident, direct. You've earned the right to give a definitive answer.]**

**VOICEOVER:**

Okay. Here's the actual answer you came for.

There's no single "best AI." But there *is* a best AI for you — and it depends on what you do most.

**If you write for a living** — content, emails, marketing, anything — use Claude. It writes like a human and thinks before it speaks.

**If you code** — use ChatGPT. Specifically, set up Cursor or GitHub Copilot running on GPT-4o and you will move faster than you thought possible.

**If you work in Google's ecosystem** — Gmail, Docs, Sheets, Search — use Gemini. The integration is genuinely useful and the real-time web access saves you from embarrassing yourself with outdated information.

**If you need to analyze large documents** — legal contracts, research reports, transcripts — Claude is in a different league.

**If you need deep reasoning on complex problems** — strategy, analysis, multi-step logic — ChatGPT o3 is the best tool we have right now.

Here's the thing most people get wrong: you don't have to pick one. The people getting the most out of AI in 2025 aren't the ones who found their favorite — they're the ones who know which tool to reach for and when.

A carpenter doesn't use a hammer for everything. Neither should you.
